Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

In this post, I discuss what neuroplasticity and growth mindset are, and how they relate to my learning journey at Dev Academy.

How do people benefit from understanding the principles of neuroplasticity?

Neuroplasticity refers to the brain's ability to change structurally and functionally in response to various experiences throughout our lives. Everytime we learn something new or undergo a new experience, the neurons in our brain form new or stronger connections with one another. This enables us to adapt to different experiences, such as rehabilitation after an injury.

A great starting point to understand neuroplasticity is by listening to Dr Lara Boyd's TEDx Talk. She explains that long-term memory occurs when new connections are formed between neurons, thus changing the structure of the brain. But these connections can be formed only through long-term repetitive experiences or actions of a certain intensity. The more we practice the stronger the neural connections become until we do an action or behave in a certain way automatically. Now we know why "practice makes perfect"!

When we understand that our brain is changeable, we develop a growth mindset, which motivates us to learn a new skill, break a habit, or modify our behaviour through regular practice. This is in contrast to having a fixed mindset where one believes they have been born with a static set of traits, including character and intelligence. They attribute their achievements to these qualities and therefore, view failure as a deficiency of own's abilities. This makes them feel the need to continuously prove themselves to show their abilities are good enough.

What are some ways to increase neuroplasticity?

  • Sleep: Perhaps the most important is sleep! Regular adequate sleep is necessary to strengthen important neural connections and weaken irrelevant ones, as revealed in this study at the University of Cambridge.
  • Focus: Paying attention makes you an effective learner according to Dr Michael Merzenich. This ties in well with the concept of mindfulness, which we have been practising at Dev Academy.
  • Exercise: Regular exercise stimulates the growth of brain cells and neuronal connections (from scientificamerican)

How is growth mindset relevant?

This lovely animation for children summarises how a growth mindset can help you achieve things in life. The lessons learnt are relevant not only to learning but for all aspects of our lives.

  • Chapter 1: Our brain is changeable. Solving hard problems makes you smarter.
  • Chapter 2: It's ok to make mistakes. We just need to learn from them.
  • Chapter 3: Finding a task difficult or having to ask for help doesn't make you dumb. Say "not yet".
  • Chapter 4: Learn challenging things.
  • Chapter 5: Learn to love challenges. Get a good night's sleep. Take small steps.

When applying our growth mindset to learning, it is important to understand that neuroplasticity is variable between people, i.e., the amount of practice required can vary between people striving to master the the same activity. So one must not compare one's learning with others. For instance, when learning to drive a car for the first time, one person could master the skills with 50 hours of driving practice, while someone else would require 200 hours.

How would you engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your own benefit?

As I now understand the principles of neuroplasticity and how to enhance it, I can apply them to my learning at Dev Academy and in other aspects of my life. In the case of web development, I will ensure I build my technical competence through daily practice of adequate dosage, while being a mindful learning. I normally pay attention to getting adequate sleep and exercise, and will continue to do so.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey? In this exploration, did anything change you?

The lessons I have learnt from my research on neuroplasticity and growth mindset have certainly made my attitude towards learning more positive. In particular, I will remember the power of "yet". If I fail to find the solution to a task, I must not be dejected or feel dumb. Instead, I can use problem solving techniques to review my work, and reach out for help to find a solution. Clearly, learning and practising through challenging tasks are the stepping stones to success.
